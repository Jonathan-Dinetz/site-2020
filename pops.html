OVERVIEW

Piedmont for Oakland Public Schools (POPS) is a non profit organization founded by a group of friends and I home from school during the summer of 2020. The goal of the organization is to leverage the privilege and resources of our hometown, Piedmont, to fight racial inequity in education, both inside and outside the “bubble” of Piedmont. POPS runs campaigns to raise money for everything from laptops and wifi to providing agricultural education that helps former inmates learn sustainable skills. To date, we have raised over $85,000 for such causes. Within Piedmont POPS works to facilitate positive cultural change by hosting events that emphasize a wider range of racial perspectives within Piedmont’s predominantly white school system.

CHALLENGE

The POPS project began with us asking what role a white, liberal, upper-class suburb could play in the Black Lives Matter movement, and the fight against systemic racism. It was clear that Piedmont’s most valuable asset was its resources. In order to unlock the town’s full power in that arena, we would have to create trust between the adults in Piedmont—a population of doctors, lawyers and chief executive officers—and us: 5 teenagers who had never raised a cent.

On top of that we needed to work quickly, especially in our first campaign. We feared that the movement and protests surrounding the murders of George Flloyd, Breonna Taylor, and many others would lose steam like so many before it, taking the attention and urgency of Piedmonters down with it. We had to be economical in our strategic infrastructure and brutally decisive in our choice of supporting materials.

Finally, we had a pandemic to work around. We could not host large in-person promotional events to get the word out about our new organization. Most of our introductions and promotions would be happening online, where the competition for eyeballs is more fierce and emotion harder to convey than in person.

SOLUTION

We built an identity that aimed to highlight both the urgency of our cause, through the use of powerful, black and white photography, and also a sense of “put-together-ness”—essential to building trust with donors—through an adherence to strict graphic standards of type, color, and layout.

On our website we built a leaderboard for donations, displaying names of leading and recent donors, which plays off the competitive nature of the Piedmont community. When showing the site around, the leaderboard always grabbed a user’s attention, with parents and parents friends being driven to out-do one another.

On social media we used motion and bright yellow backgrounds in every post in order to more efficiently display type-heavy messages, catch eyes, and burst through the clutter of the typical social media feed. We didn’t conduct formal research on the results of our social media presence, but when discussing our campaigns with some of the high schoolers in town, we were immediately recognized as “the yellow account”, indicating our strategy’s success in breaking through to the inundated brain of the social media user.

Both our campaigns far exceeded our expectations, each raising tens of thousands of dollars to combat racial inequity in education throughout the East Bay. Some online campaign events have hosted over 250 Piedmonters, which have helped build understanding and continued the fight against racial bias inside and outside Piedmont.
