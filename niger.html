Overview

Access to pharmaceutical products in sub-saharan africa is a major deciding factor in health and quality of life in a particular region. Oftentimes pharmacies may be poorly stocked to handle the needs of the community it serves, but even when that need is taken care of, pharmacies can still be difficult to access as a result of cultural, economic, or infrastructural conflicts. Working with a group of 3 other students in the Brown Computer Science Department, we have developed Lahiya: a mobile app for Niger designed to address such secondary conflicts.

Challenge

If you find yourself with a splitting headache after dark in Nigerâ€™s capital city, Niamey, you will be faced with the challenge of finding an open pharmacy. This is because Niger rotates what pharmacies are open 24 hrs/day on a completely random schedule. This schedule is not readily available to the public, leading to frustration, a reluctance to seek out medication when needed, and ultimately a lower quality of healthcare in the country.

We set out to design an app that not only addresses the issue of unpredictable hours, but also one that makes the process of acquiring medication in a country with immense linguistic and cultural diversity.

Solution

In our initial explorations, we centered the app around a map. It is built for use at night with a dark background, so as not blind users while navigating. We also tried to find the proper balance of landmarks to include. We tried to limit the clutter that usually faces a user on a map application by including only the elements relevant to finding pharmacies (streets and distinctive landmarks only).

For illiterate users (or users who do not speak one of the major Nigerien languages), an option to scan a particular medication with a phone camera and find pharmacies nearby that stock it using entirely iconographic navigation is provided.

Finally, we have included a third option which allows users to search for medication by ailment. This again employs iconography very directly, in an effort to provide an entirely visual user flow.

This application is still under development, and user studies have been limited to phone calls as grants from Brown allowing our team to travel to Niger and conduct more deliberate studies were cancelled due to COVID-19.
